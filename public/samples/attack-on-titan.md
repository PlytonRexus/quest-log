The series fundamentally shifts what it is about every season without ever feeling like a betrayal of its premise. What starts as a survival horror about giant monsters eating people becomes a political thriller, then a war story examining cycles of violence from multiple sides.

The mystery structure is the strongest element. Isayama plants details in early episodes that only pay off dozens of chapters later: the basement, the coordinate, the history of the walls. Each revelation recontextualizes everything that came before rather than just adding new information.

Eren's character arc is divisive but narratively coherent. He begins as a rage-fueled protagonist with a simple goal, and the story systematically strips away the simplicity until he becomes something the audience is no longer sure they should root for. The shift from protagonist to something more ambiguous is handled gradually enough that you can trace each step.

The Survey Corps sequences deliver on the action front with the ODM gear creating a unique visual language for combat. Levi's fights in particular are choreographed with a precision that makes them rewatchable in isolation. However, the show is at its best when the action serves character moments, like Erwin's charge or the ocean scene.
