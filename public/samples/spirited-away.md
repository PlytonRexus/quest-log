Miyazaki builds a world that operates on its own dream logic, where every rule feels internally consistent even when nothing is explained. Chihiro's transformation from a whiny child into someone capable of standing her ground is compelling because it happens gradually, through labor and small acts of kindness rather than a single heroic moment.

The bathhouse functions as both a literal workplace and a metaphor for growing up. Chihiro must learn names, navigate hierarchies, and earn respect from people who initially dismiss her. No-Face is particularly fascinating as a character who mirrors whatever environment he occupies, shifting from lonely outsider to consuming monster depending on who feeds him attention.

The film rewards rewatching. Details that seem decorative on first viewing turn out to be thematically loaded: the river, the pig transformation, the train ride across the water. Haku's arc runs parallel to Chihiro's but in reverse, recovering an identity that was taken rather than building a new one.
